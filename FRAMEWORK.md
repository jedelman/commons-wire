# The Framework

This document defines the analytical framework used by commons-wire to score
and write about news items. It's here so forks can substitute their own.

---

## The core insight

Every system that captures value — platforms, corporations, states, media —
depends on a productive substrate it cannot produce itself: **the common**.
Shared language, knowledge, care, attention, social cooperation. The system
can enclose and extract from the common. It cannot simulate it. The common
continuously generates more than any capture mechanism can contain. That
excess is where alternatives live.

## The capture sequence

Capture operates at escalating levels of immanence:

1. **Crude coercion** — do this or face consequences
2. **Manufacturing consent** — make people want the thing that makes the system work
3. **Aesthetic capture** — absorb the forms of resistance into the market
4. **Simulation** — replace the real with a managed representation of the real
5. **Encoding capture into desire** — when you want the thing that makes the system function

Each stage is harder to see and harder to resist using the tools of the previous stage.

## The two registers

Most resistance operates in the **representational register** — new content,
new messaging, new faces, new brands. This is capturable.

Resistance that operates in the **flow register** — building different routing
architectures, different organizational forms, different productive circuits —
is structurally harder to capture.

The key question for any story: **is this a content-level change or a
routing-level change?**

## Analytical tools

Use the right one for the story.

**Alibi structure**
What does this system, narrative, or policy require you to believe in order to
seem legitimate? That thing is its alibi. When the alibi becomes visible as an
alibi, the system becomes temporarily illegible.

**Fatal strategy**
When a system takes its own logic past the point of functionality into
self-parody or self-destruction.

**Simulation collision**
Two mutually reinforcing narratives collide — each was the other's alibi —
and both become simultaneously untenable.

**Routing architecture**
Don't ask what something says. Ask what it routes. Who does it move? Where
does attention flow? What connections does it make possible or impossible?

**Epistemic incompetence of centralization**
Centralized systems systematically destroy the distributed intelligence
required to know complex systems. Not corruption, not stupidity: structural
epistemic failure.

**The commune layer**
Where are people routing flows of care, knowledge, and resources through each
other rather than through market or state intermediaries?

**Durability test**
Is this a genuine political moment or manufactured urgency? Genuine dialectical
images keep returning. Manufactured urgency dissipates in hours.

## What high-relevance looks like

A story scores high (7-10) if it:
- Shows the capture mechanism in operation (alibi becoming visible, fatal
  strategy, routing question)
- Shows the commune layer building, defending, or being attacked
- Shows distributed intelligence being destroyed by centralization (or surviving it)
- Shows the gendered, raced, or classed nature of enclosure or extraction
- Shows a concrete example of prefigurative institution-building

A story scores low (1-4) if it:
- Is primarily representational (new face, new brand, new message, new scandal)
- Is manufactured urgency (horse-race politics, celebrity, outrage cycle)
- Requires the framework to be forced onto it
- Is adequately explained by simpler analysis

## Writing principles

- Open with the specific story. One concrete event, headline, or moment.
- Say what most coverage is missing.
- Apply one or two tools from the framework. Not all of them.
- Land on something actionable or generative.
- 400–700 words. Never padded.
- Analytical but not cold. Point of view is legitimate. Not polemical.
- No academic jargon. If you use a concept, translate it in the same sentence.
- Fact-check every specific claim before publishing.
